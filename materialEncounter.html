Triangulate ‘Listening’ to Materials

Michael Nitsche, Jialuo Yang
Abstract: To listen to materials is an often-repeated claim in design, yet methodolo- gies on how to perform such a task are less clear. First, this paper will review forms of material encounters by leaning on more-than-human design and hybrid craft. It asks how the concept of “listening” changed with the emergence of material agencies. Second, it will suggest its core approach: to focus not on the human-material encoun- ter but to stage a material-material encounter that opens up a relational “triangu- lated listening” position. Third, it will provide an example case showing possible ap- proaches for such a third-party listening set up. Overall, it contributes a method that informs one form of listening to materials in design through theory and practice.
Case Study

The following design project serves as a practical example for the theoretical triangulation model outlined above. In terms of its development, the study followed initially established trajectories in hybrid craft design. It started with close observations of a material (step 1) through personal encounters. These included use of the material experiential design tool kit by Camere/Karana (Camere & Karana, 2018). It also included an autoethnographic exercise during the papermaking, which included soma-design methods (Tsaknaki et al., 2019), such as body sheets. In the second step, the designer conducted practice observations as they vis- ited an active crafter working with the material. This required observation of how to handle the materials and tools in specific environments (step 2). Informed by these experiences, the designer ideated a material encounter between the chosen first and a second material – the triangulation of listening proposed here (step 3). Finally, based on the nature of that encoun- ter the digital intervention was designed and implemented (step 4). The first two steps are to establish forms of personal observation of material properties in relation to oneself and the practice of others in relation to the material (Sennett, 2008). These are necessary steps to approach the materials and the practices surrounding them. Step 3 features the concep- tual shift outlined above. Instead of building directly on this emerging human-material en- counter, the triangulation took the designer out of immediate contact and emphasized ma- terial relationships first. Those developing relationships depended on the materials’ own conditions/ activities/ timeframe. They emphasize material encounters that a human would most likely not realize. Emphasizing these material conditions first allowed the subsequent design of a digital intervention based on a novel “listening” position that remained aware of a human experience of the material but shifted it toward the material-material enactment first. Finally, these designs were implemented and operated (step 4). The novel human “lis- tening” emerged in this operation and operating the prototypes is essential. The design em- phasized the material-material relation first and put the human in a novel position towards this encounter. Given the focus on the triangulation model, here we will report on this com- ponent and will merely outline the initial material encounter and practice exploration steps.

The Active Paper case study focused on paper as the material. Key material encounters with paper involve the experience with its texture, folding/ creases, and physical behavior. Paper- making practices were observed with a local crafter and translated into own experiments at papermaking. Typically, the process of papermaking can be categorized into various steps such as processing fiber, making pulp, screening, compression, and dehydration. Each of these steps alters the emerging properties such as transparency, roughness, flatness, thick- ness, smoothness, and color of the emerging paper (Hunter, 1978 (1947)). They affect the paper’s properties and specific agencies. Herein, different instances of paper were created for this project to allow for a range of these features.

Triangulation was achieved via inclusion of a second material encounter. This encounter cen- tered on the moment when a pen would touch the paper surface. Human experience of this encounter is often neglected or uniform. If the paper is optimized for a smooth writing expe- rience this encounter might go unnoticed. If a paper or pen creates a specific, constant feel- ing during the writing experience, this encounter would stay consistent and predictable. Countering this lack of perception, the project focused on this encounter of the tip of a pen with a paper as a unique and constantly emerging activity between two active material com- ponents acting upon each other. The digital intervention prototype, then, focused on bring- ing this moment forward to the human participant.
